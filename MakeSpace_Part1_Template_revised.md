## Notice

### Open with a practitioner

*Begin in the middle of something specific. Not "this week we examine X" — more like: [Practitioner] did this. Here is what that looked like.*

*The opening image should do immediate work: concrete, a little surprising, and carrying the week's core question without naming it yet. The reader should feel something before they understand anything.*

[2–3 sentences. Describe one specific moment, method, gesture, or discovery from one of this week's three practitioners. What did they actually do, make, or find? Name the work, the session, the material, the study — whatever is most specific and most arresting. If the practitioner wrote something, describe what they wrote about, not what they concluded. If they made something, describe what they made, not what it meant.]

*→ Integrity note: This opening image must be traceable to a verifiable source — a published book, a documented session, a peer-reviewed study, a recorded talk, or a known body of work. Do not invent details. If you cannot name a specific source, describe the work in general terms or choose a different practitioner.*

### Name the week's question — obliquely

[1–2 sentences. A short pivot from the opening image toward the territory this week is in. Not a thesis statement — more like a direction of travel. Something like: "That is where this week begins: with [brief restatement of the tension the practitioner just embodied]."]

### The framework — held lightly

*Bring in the research, the theoretical context, the Make Space hypothesis. But keep it compressed and in motion — this is not a literature review. The framework exists to name something the practitioner has already shown. Move through it in a paragraph or two.*

[2–3 paragraphs. Draw on the relevant evidence base for this week's territory — neuroscience, psychology, creative health research, somatic theory, community arts literature, whatever the week calls for. Where a finding is contested, partial, or carries documented limitations, say so inside the prose — not as a staged aside, not as a bracketed note. Honest uncertainty belongs to the same sentence as the claim it qualifies. The goal is a voice that already knows the limits of its maps.]

*→ Integrity note: Every empirical claim must be traceable to a named source. If you cannot name the source, soften the claim or remove it. Do not invent caveats to seem balanced — fabricated criticism is as damaging as uncritical acceptance.*

### A second practitioner (or deeper into the first)

[2–3 sentences or a short paragraph. Bring in a second practitioner from this week's three — or go deeper with the first. Give the work space. What did they discover, make, practise, or argue? Where their reception is contested, or where the work sits inside a live debate, name that honestly — woven into the description, not appended to it.]

*→ Integrity note: If a debate around this practitioner's work is unresolved, leave it unresolved. If you cannot name who objected and what they said, describe the tension in the work itself rather than manufacturing a critical controversy.*

*Suggested length for full Notice section: 300–400 words.*

*Note on voice: Make Space speaks from inside the practice, not above it. The "I" of this project is a maker-in-process. Write from that position — curious, grounded, willing to be uncertain.*

## Voice checklist

Before you post:

- [ ] Does it open with a specific image from a practitioner's work, not a concept or a framework?
- [ ] Is the framework present but not dominant — does it arrive after the practitioner, not before?
- [ ] Where research or theory is contested, is that said directly inside the prose — not as a bracketed aside, and not invented for the appearance of balance?
- [ ] Where a practitioner's reception is contested, is the debate documented and specific — named critics, named positions — rather than constructed?
- [ ] Does the piece resist explaining what the practice means, and instead let it do its own work?
- [ ] Is there a single unifying question or tension that holds the whole piece together — felt rather than stated?
- [ ] Has every empirical claim been traced to a named source? Has every citation in the Anchors been verified against the actual text?
- [ ] Have any AI-generated biographical details, artist statements, or critical positions been verified against a primary source before including?
