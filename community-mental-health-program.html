Make Space as a Community Mental Health Program

A reference for understanding the program, its place in Global Mental Health, and what a responsible path to scale would require.

Make Space: Other Bodies and Creative Health began as a fifty-two-week creative curriculum — a personal investigation into what happens when a person is given permission to make without a goal, with the natural world kept close as a companion for feeling. This document describes what that practice becomes when it is deliberately reframed as a community mental health program, and what would be required for it to grow, ethically and sustainably, toward a Global Mental Health initiative for reaching people whom early depression care does not currently reach.

The one line that governs everything

Make Space is not therapy and does not treat or cure depression. It is a preventive, supportive, early-access layer — a first rung people can reach before, alongside, or when they cannot get clinical care — and a pathway toward support, never a substitute for it. Hold this line and the rest becomes possible. Blur it and the program becomes both unethical and unfundable.

A note on evidence discipline

This document follows Make Space's own fact-checking standard. Claims are calibrated to what the evidence actually supports; "evidence-informed" is preferred to "evidence-based" until the evidence is the program's own; and anything not yet verified against a primary source carries a verify flag rather than being stated as settled fact.

The Program

What Make Space Is

The intervention beneath the program — its content, its mechanism, and what makes it distinctive.

At its centre is a simple, disciplined practice: goal-less making — creative acts with no requirement to be good or finished — carried out inside a supportive structure that keeps the natural world close as a mirror for human feeling. The medium is watercolour, chosen precisely because it resists control; staying present while the paint pools and spreads is itself the practice. Every session begins and ends with somatic arrival: noticing the weight of the body and the breath before and after making.

The Other Bodies framework

The signature contribution is Other Bodies: fifty-two feelings mapped across a year, each set beside one natural body and one small act of making. Animals, plants, and weather systems are treated as "sites of wisdom" rather than metaphors — bodies that have practised difficult states without pathologising them. Shame met through the exquisite sensitivity of the octopus; anger through the storm that redistributes pressure and dissipates; longing through the wolf's howl that locates the pack. The feelings are offered in pencil, not ink — a guess held loosely, never a diagnosis.

Why this matters for a mental health program

Because Other Bodies works through nature and making rather than through diagnostic language, it offers a somatic, non-verbal doorway for people whom words — and clinical framing — have failed or excluded. This is exactly the population early depression care most often misses.

What it proposes (the working hypothesis)

Creative health is best cultivated when individuals are given explicit permission to fail — through goal-less making — within a highly supportive structure that uses the natural world as a mirror for the complexities of human feeling. The program treats this as a genuine research question, investigated in the open, where the making is the data.

The Program

From Curriculum to Program

The single reframe that turns a personal practice into a community mental health intervention.

The move is not to add clinical machinery. It is to change what the thing understands itself to be: from content delivered to an audience, to a documented, community-based early intervention in which the participant is a co-investigator of their own creative health, and the program itself is studied as it runs.

The two shifts that cost nothing

  • The participant becomes a co-investigator, not an audience
    The year-long question — what happens when people give themselves permission to make without a goal? — is genuinely a research question. The participant gathers evidence in their own life, week by week. The making is the data.
  • Other Bodies becomes the living case study and instrument set
    The emotional compass, the constellations, the emotion stories are not illustrations. They are original conceptual instruments for mapping the inner world — and naming them that way changes how the whole program is read by a partner or a researcher.

    • What must be protected in the move

    The gravest failure mode is becoming clinical at the cost of being kind. Three strengths are particular to Make Space and must not be flattened by borrowed structure:

    Original instruments
    The Other Bodies tools are genuinely novel — potentially the program's first publishable innovation. Protect them as signature contribution.
    A somatic, non-verbal entry
    Reaching people through making and the body, not argument — a doorway for those whom words have failed.
    A live, gathered community
    The real-time social layer that most task-shifting models and publications structurally struggle to sustain.
    The value rule

    When something must be cut, cut from the front — the concept — never from the back: the making, sharing, and rest. The made thing and the rest are the medicine; the concept is only the doorway. At scale the temptation is to ship cheap "content about creative health" and quietly thin the guided making. Resist it with fidelity checks.

    The Program

    The Four Layers

    From the individual outward to the globe — four concentric layers, each a real deliverable.

    Layer 1 — The Practice

    The 52-week arc is the content spine. For a community program it becomes modular: the full year for depth, and a bounded 12-week "core dose" (the Foundations arc plus two or three Other Bodies constellations) that a partner site can actually run and study. Task-shifting needs a teachable, bounded protocol; a year is a way of life, twelve weeks is a program.

    Layer 2 — The Group

    The ohHEY Creative Care Community, formalised into the facilitated circle: 6–12 people, a trained non-specialist facilitator, a weekly rhythm of somatic arrival → goal-less making → invited sharing → rest. The group is the mechanism — belonging and connection are the most evidence-supported ingredients in the whole model.

    Layer 3 — The Site

    A partner-hosted home for circles — a library, a clinic waiting room, a school, a refugee support centre, a rural health post. Following the community-engagement model in the Araya/TAPS literature, each site is built through a pre-engagement phase: local organisations and leaders develop a shared understanding of local need before a single circle runs. The site handles referral both ways — receiving people via social prescribing, and routing anyone in distress out to clinical care.

    Layer 4 — The Network

    Sites federated into a network that shares the protocol, the training, the documentation standard, and the evidence — while each locale adapts the content to its culture. Lightweight federation, not heavy central control; local ownership of content and data.

    The design principle across all four

    Scale the trained human relationship and the honest evidence at the same rate as the content. The program is only as strong as its weakest layer — and the weakest layer is almost always the one where a human facilitator was replaced by a document.

    Where It Sits & Scope

    Locating Make Space precisely in the system of care — and drawing the boundary that keeps it safe.

    The scope boundary

    Facilitators hold space for making and feeling. They do not assess, diagnose, counsel, or treat. The single non-negotiable competency is recognising when someone is beyond the program's scope — signs of crisis, acute risk, or clinical-level depression — and knowing exactly where to route them.

    Make Space is
    Preventive · early-access · supportive · adjunctive · a pathway to care · a place to be met in a feeling · a low-threshold community doorway
    Make Space is not
    Therapy · a treatment for depression · a diagnosis · a crisis service · a substitute for clinical care · a replacement for a professional
    Standing care note (belongs everywhere)

    A short, kind, current note — what this is, what it isn't, and where to turn if today is hard — belongs visibly on the program and near anything that touches distress. A creative-health program without a live route to clinical care is not an early intervention; it is an abdication.

    Critical questions

    The Big Questions

    The questions a participant, a partner, a funder, or an ethics board will ask — answered directly and honestly.

    What actually is it — in one sentence a stranger would understand?
    A community-based, non-clinical creative program in which small facilitated groups make art without a goal, using the natural world as a companion for difficult feelings, as an early and preventive layer of mental health support that also connects people to care.
    Who is it for?
    People experiencing low mood, isolation, subthreshold or mild depressive symptoms, or the "difficulty that has no name yet" — especially those whom clinical services miss or reach too late. It is designed to be accessible to people excluded from both creative spaces and mental health services. It is not for people in acute crisis or with severe, active illness, who need clinical care the program refers them toward.
    Does it work? What's the evidence?
    The honest answer: the broader field of arts-and-health has a real and growing evidence base (the 2019 WHO scoping review synthesised hundreds of studies), and arts engagement is associated with reduced depressive symptoms and improved wellbeing. But that evidence is largely associational, and Make Space's specific model has not yet been trialled. So the defensible claim today is "evidence-informed and promising," not "proven." Generating the program's own evidence is a core task, not an afterthought.
    Is it therapy? Is it safe?
    It is explicitly not therapy, and saying so protects both the participant and the program. Safety rests on four things: a bright scope boundary, a live two-way referral pathway to clinical care, trauma-aware design (invitation not instruction; consent and pace honoured; a visible door to support), and clinical oversight in the governance structure.
    How could it possibly scale globally?
    Through task-shifting: training non-specialist community members to deliver a bounded, teachable protocol under supervision. This is a well-established mechanism in global mental health. The critical caveat is that the strongest task-shifting evidence is for structured brief psychological treatments — not creative approaches — so Make Space scales first as a preventive/early-access layer while it builds its own evidence toward any stronger claim.
    How is a program like this funded and kept alive?
    Through diversified funding tied to an evidence ladder: quality data → qualitative paper → feasibility study → pilot → controlled evidence, each stage unlocking the next tier of funder (philanthropy, health-system social-prescribing budgets, global-health grants). The Araya/TAPS experience names the four things that kill programs like this — weak financial sustainability, administrative burden, poor inter-organisational coordination, and the digital divide — so sustainability is designed against those four by name.
    Won't a Western emotional model harm the cultures it enters?
    It can, and that is the field's most common failure (Kleinman's central warning). The safeguard is structural: the framework travels, but the specific natural companions, idioms, and feelings are co-created locally with each community, which also owns its own content and data. Depression is experienced differently across cultures, and the program must be adapted, not translated.
    What's the single thing most likely to go wrong?
    That the claim outruns the proof, or the content scales faster than the trained human care — and someone in real distress is held by a circle instead of routed to help. Every structural safeguard in this document exists to prevent that one outcome.
    Why creative health at all — why not just fund more clinicians?
    Because there will never be enough specialists to close the gap, especially in low-resource settings, and because many people never reach clinical services for reasons of stigma, access, cost, or design. A low-threshold, culturally adaptable, community-owned doorway reaches people the specialist system structurally cannot — not instead of clinical care, but as the wide base beneath it.
    What does success look like in five years?
    Not "millions treated." Realistically: a validated, manualised core dose; a trained-and-supervised facilitator corps; feasibility and early outcome evidence published honestly; two or more culturally distinct sites co-owning their adaptations; and a funding base that isn't a single grant. Depth and integrity first; reach follows from proof.

    The Treatment Gap

    The stakes that give Make Space its purpose — the distance between who needs care and who receives it.

    Depression is one of the leading causes of disability worldwide, affecting hundreds of millions of people. Yet a large majority of those who need care never receive it — and the gap is widest exactly where resources are thinnest.

    The gap is not primarily a diagnostic problem. It is a problem of access, resource, workforce, and design. Over 80% of the world's population lives in low- and middle-income countries, which receive a small fraction of global mental health resources — a disparity Kleinman frames not only as inequality but as a violation of basic rights.

    Why this shapes the whole program

    Make Space operates inside this context — not as a clinical solution, but as part of the evidential case that structured creative approaches can reach people whom specialist systems cannot. The treatment gap is not an abstraction; it is a design failure with a human cost. That awareness should shape how the program is built: for the people currently left out, in the settings where care is hardest to reach.

    Global Mental Health

    Task-Shifting

    The mechanism that lets non-specialists deliver care where specialists don't exist — and its honest limits for creative approaches.

    Task-shifting (also "task-sharing") means training and supervising non-specialist workers — community members, peers, facilitators — to deliver simplified, structured mental health interventions. It is the central strategy behind scaling mental health care in resource-limited settings, developed most influentially through Vikram Patel's work and the WHO's mhGAP framework.

    What the evidence supports

    Trials of task-shifted, manualised psychological treatments — such as brief behavioural interventions delivered by lay counsellors — have shown that non-specialists can deliver effective depression care when they are trained, supervised, and working from a structured protocol. Supervision is the quiet key: the model works when non-specialists are supported, not when they are trained and abandoned.

    The honest caveat for Make Space

    Calibrate this carefully

    The strongest task-shifting evidence is for structured brief psychological treatments, not for creative approaches. So Make Space cannot yet claim to be an evidence-based, task-shifted treatment for depression. Two defensible paths, likely in sequence: (a) scale it as a preventive / early-access / adjunctive layer, where the evidence bar and the ethics are cleaner; and (b) generate its own trial evidence so that, one day, a stronger claim is earned rather than borrowed.

    Global Mental Health

    Creative Health & Social Prescribing

    The field Make Space lives inside — its evidence base and the policy mechanism that can carry it.

    Creative health

    "Creative health" describes the use of the arts and creative engagement to support health and wellbeing — broader and more person-centred than clinical art therapy. The anchor evidence is the 2019 WHO scoping review by Daisy Fancourt and Saoirse Finn, which synthesised a large body of studies on the arts and health. The field is real and growing, and also young: parts of it are thinner than its enthusiasts would like. Both are true at once, and a responsible program holds them together.

    What's supported
    Arts engagement is associated with reduced depressive symptoms, improved social connection, and physiological markers such as lower stress hormones.calibrate
    What isn't
    That creative practice "cures" or "treats" depression. The strength of every claim must match what the cited source actually says.

    Social prescribing

    Social prescribing is the practice of referring people from primary care to non-medical, community-based activities — including creative programs. It is embedded in the NHS and spreading internationally. It matters to Make Space in two directions: it is the referral mechanism that sends people in, and it is the policy category that funders and health systems already recognise. A program built to be "the kind of thing social prescribing sends people to" has a ready-made route to legitimacy and funding.

    Positioning

    Make Space is a living case study inside the creative-health evidence base and a natural destination for social prescribing. Mapping it explicitly against these two frames is how it becomes legible to partners who have never heard of Other Bodies.

    Global Mental Health

    Cultural Humility

    Arthur Kleinman's warning, and why it is structural rather than decorative for any global effort.

    Kleinman — psychiatrist and anthropologist — reshaped global mental health by insisting that mental illness cannot be separated from the cultural and social contexts in which it occurs. Depression is experienced, expressed, and understood differently across cultures: through the body, through spiritual frames, through idioms that do not map onto Western diagnostic categories.

    The concepts that matter here

  • Explanatory models
    People hold their own models of what their suffering is and what it needs. Care that ignores those models miscommunicates and fails. A program must meet people inside their own understanding.
  • Social suffering
    Distress is shaped by social and structural conditions, not only individual pathology. This aligns closely with Make Space's community, non-medicalising stance.
  • Critique of psychiatric globalisation
    Exporting a Western model and universalising it can flatten the very cultures it claims to serve. The alternative is context-specific, community-based care.

    • Why Other Bodies is unusually well-suited — if handled right

    Because the framework works through nature and making rather than diagnostic language, it can travel across cultures more gently than a symptom checklist. But this only holds if the natural companions are locally chosen. The octopus, the permafrost, the storm are one culture's companions; another community's will be its own animals, its own weather, its own plants, named in its own idioms. The framework travels; the specific companions must be co-created locally.

    The line between global program and cultural colonialism

    Communities co-create their own companions and own their own content and data — including any ancestral or traditional knowledge, which is attributed to its source community or left out entirely if it cannot be properly attributed. This is not a courtesy. It is the ethical condition of operating globally at all.

    Building it

    Ethical Architecture

    For a program that names depression, ethics is not a compliance chore — it is the product. Built first, not bolted on.

  • Scope of practice & the referral spine
    Facilitators hold space; they do not assess, diagnose, or treat. The core competency is recognising when someone is beyond scope and knowing exactly where to route them. This referral pathway must be mapped, current, and locally real at every site before it opens.
  • Emotional-safety design
    Offer a feeling, never instruct it; honour pace and consent; sharing is invitation, never requirement; trauma-aware prompts that offer outs and never force memory or exposure; a visible standing note pointing to real support. At scale these become trained behaviours with a fidelity checklist.
  • Claims discipline
    "Evidence-informed," not "evidence-based," until the evidence is the program's own. Supportive and preventive, never curative. Every claim calibrated to what its source actually supports. This honesty is also the program's credibility moat with serious funders.
  • Consent & data ethics
    Once making becomes data, participants must know it, agree to it, and be able to withdraw it. Informed consent, anonymisation, secure storage — and, at global scale, data sovereignty: communities own their own data.
  • Cultural humility as design
    Adapt, don't translate. Local companions, local idioms, local ownership. The framework travels; the specifics are co-created.
  • Born-accessible
    Every interactive tool ships with a linear, screen-reader-and-keyboard companion. For a program claiming to reach people the system misses, accessibility is part of the ethical claim, not a feature.
    • The formal layer

    Before scaling beyond a first pilot: research-ethics / IRB review, a legal and governance structure (nonprofit, or hosted by an existing health organisation), safeguarding policies, data-protection compliance appropriate to each jurisdiction, and named clinical oversight. These convert a beautiful practice into a defensible program.

    Building it

    The Facilitator Model

    The operational heart — how ordinary community members are trained to hold a circle safely.

    The facilitator is where task-shifting becomes real. The training is not about making people junior therapists; it is about equipping them to hold a specific, bounded rhythm with warmth, and to recognise the edge of their scope.

    What a facilitator is trained to do

    CompetencyWhat it looks like
    Hold the rhythmSomatic arrival → goal-less making → invited sharing → rest, week over week, with fidelity to the protocol
    Safety behavioursInvitation not instruction; consent and pace; trauma-aware prompting; a visible route to support
    Recognise the edgeSpot signs of crisis, acute risk, or clinical-level distress — and know this is beyond scope
    Refer, both waysRoute people out to clinical care; receive people in via social prescribing
    Document with consentKeep the reflective notes that become the program's evidence — ethically and consistently
    Hold the boundaryNever assess, diagnose, counsel, or treat; name clearly what the circle is and isn't

    The training pathway

  • Experience the practice first
    A facilitator completes the core dose as a participant before training to hold it. You cannot facilitate a doorway you have not walked through.
  • Learn the protocol and the safety spine
    The manual, the fidelity checklist, and — above all — the referral/escalation protocol and the scope boundary.
  • Practise under supervision
    Co-facilitate, then facilitate with observation and feedback. Supervision continues after training, not just during it.
  • Ongoing support
    Regular supervision, a community of facilitators, and clear escalation to clinical oversight. This is what separates safe task-shifting from abandonment.
    • The non-negotiable

    No circle runs anywhere until the local referral pathway is mapped, current, and real, and clinical oversight is named. The safety spine is built before the first session, not after the first incident.

    Building it

    Evidence & Sustainability

    The same act, done twice — documentation solves the research problem and the funding problem together.

    The reflective journal after each session, the end-of-phase feedback, the careful notes on what the Other Bodies tools produce — kept consistently, at scale, with consent — become, in ascending order, the program's evidence and its funding case at once.

    The evidence ladder

  • Internal quality data
    Consistent documentation from day one — before you need it.
  • Qualitative paper
    What participants experience, in their own voices, analysed honestly.
  • Feasibility study
    Can the model be delivered as designed, safely, in a real setting?
  • Pilot with validated measures
    A brief depression screen used as a wellbeing signal and referral trigger, not a diagnosis.
  • Controlled evidence
    The trial evidence a serious global funder or health system eventually requires.

    • Sustainability — designed against the four TAPS failure modes

    The Araya/TAPS experience is instructive precisely because of what broke it. Build against each by name:

    Named risk (from TAPS)Design response
    Weak financial sustainabilityDiversified funding across the evidence ladder — never a single grant
    Administrative burdenRadically low admin load on facilitators; simple, embedded documentation
    Poor inter-organisational coordinationLightweight federation, not heavy central control; clear local roles
    The digital divideOffline-first, low-bandwidth delivery so the tech doesn't re-exclude the target population
    Start now

    The documentation layer costs nothing to begin and compounds. By the time you want a paper, a partner, or a funder, the evidence already exists. Treat documentation as part of the practice, not admin after it.

    The Staged Pathway

    What "possible" actually looks like — each stage cheap, compounding, and producing the artifact the next stage needs.

    Stage 0
    Formalise the intervention
    Reframe, value map, series map, care note, claims guidelines, begin documentation. Needs no partner or funding.
    Stage 1
    Protocol & safety spine
    Turn the method into a teachable manual with a fidelity checklist, referral protocol, and scope boundary.
    Stage 2
    First supervised pilot
    One site, one community you know, real facilitators, real clinical oversight. Document everything with consent.
    Stage 3
    Ethics, structure, evidence
    IRB review, legal/governance structure, feasibility study with validated wellbeing measures.
    Stage 4
    Cultural adaptation
    A second, different culture; local companions co-created. The real test of whether the framework travels.

    Federation & network
    Shared protocol and evidence standard; local ownership. Map formally against WHO mhGAP and the Lancet Commission.
    The sequencing principle

    Depth and integrity precede reach. Each stage earns the next; nothing scales until it has been shown to be safe, deliverable, and honestly evidenced at the smaller size.

    Building it

    Risks & Cautions

    Four ways this breaks — named plainly, so the program can be built against them.

    1 — The claim outruns the proof
    It overstates its evidence; a partner over-relies on it in place of clinical care; someone in crisis is held by a circle instead of routed to help. Guard: the scope boundary and referral spine, enforced as the first competency and audited at every site.
    2 — Cultural flattening
    It exports a Western emotional model globally and erases the cultures it claims to serve — Kleinman's exact warning. Guard: local co-creation of companions and idioms; communities own their content and data.
    3 — Hollowing the practice at scale
    It scales the cheap content (the noticing half) while quietly thinning the making, sharing, and rest — the part that carries the value. Guard: the value rule and fidelity checklists that protect the back half of every session.
    4 — The four TAPS failure modes
    It burns out on money, admin, coordination, or the digital divide. Guard: design against each by name from Stage 0, as set out in Evidence & Sustainability.
    The whole thing in one sentence

    Make Space can become a global mental health initiative if and only if it scales the trained human relationship and the honest evidence at the same rate as the beautiful content — and never lets the claim outrun the proof, or the care outrun its scope.

    Where the evidence is still thin — and honest about it

    Open questions the program itself must help answer

    The specific Make Space model is untrialled. Creative-approach task-shifting for depression lacks the trial base that structured psychological treatments have. Physiological claims (cortisol, immune markers, DMN, mirror-neuron system) must each trace to a specific primary study, not a review — and some (e.g. mirror-neuron scope) are contested. This is not a weakness to hide; it is the research agenda that gives the program its purpose.verify

    Neuroscience & physiology claims — handle with special care

    Default Mode Network (trace to primary research, e.g. Raichle et al.), Mirror Neuron System (Rizzolatti et al.; do not describe as "proven" — contested in scope), cortisol reduction and immune function (trace to Fancourt's UCL work or the 2019 WHO report specifically). Never cite a secondary source that itself cites the study.verify

    Calibrating claim strength

    "Arts engagement is associated with reduced depression symptoms" is evidence-supported. "Creative practice cures depression" is not. Calibrate the strength of every claim to what the cited evidence actually supports — not more, not less.
