## The key

*Before the practitioner. Before the natural body. Before the research. One idea, from one thinker, that makes this week feel necessary.*

[2–3 sentences. Introduce the thinker — who they are, and from what angle they have been looking at the world. Then offer the idea, briefly. Not as a thesis statement. As a door. The reader should encounter it and feel something shift — a small recognition, a loosening — before they have understood anything. This idea should feel as though it was always true, and the reader simply hadn't had the words for it yet.]

*→ Integrity note: The thinker named here must be real, and the idea must be traceable to a specific work. Do not invent a quote. Paraphrase precisely, or mark for verification before publishing. If you cannot locate the source, choose a different thinker or write around it in general terms.*

*→ What belongs here: a philosopher, a poet, a neuroscientist at the edge of their field, an ecologist, a novelist who has named something essential about the human condition. Not another creative health researcher — the key comes from outside the field to illuminate it.*

*Suggested length: 60–100 words.*

## The practitioner

*The key has opened something. Now someone walks through the door.*

[2–3 sentences. Begin in the middle of something specific. Not "this week we examine X" — more like: [Practitioner] did this. Here is what that looked like. What did they actually do, make, or find? Name the work, the material, the session, the discovery — whatever is most specific and most arresting. The reader should feel something before they understand anything. The week's question is present here, but not yet named.]

[Optional: 1–2 sentences showing how what this practitioner did speaks to what the key was pointing toward. Not as analysis — as recognition. Let the connection surface naturally, or trust the reader to find it themselves.]

*→ Integrity note: This opening image must be traceable to a verifiable source — a published book, documented session, peer-reviewed study, recorded talk, or known body of work. Do not invent details. If you cannot name a specific source, describe the work in general terms or choose a different practitioner.*

*Suggested length: 100–150 words.*

## The natural body

[Introduce the body, the creature, the material, or the natural system in one sentence — what it is, where it lives, what condition it carries.]

**Its nature**
[2–4 sentences. Describe what this natural body actually does — its biology, its behaviour, its way of being in the world. Be specific enough that the reader can picture it, feel it, locate themselves in relation to it. No metaphor here. Just: what is true about this body?]

**What it holds**
[2–4 sentences. Name the emotional or psychological state this week pairs with this natural body. How does the natural body carry that state — not as symbol, but as lived condition? What does the octopus actually know about shame? What has permafrost genuinely practised about numbness? The hypothesis at work: natural bodies are sites of wisdom, not illustration.]

**What it reframes**
[2–4 sentences. What changes when a human looks at their own emotional experience through this natural body? What becomes possible that wasn't before? What does the reader now have permission to do, or feel, or make? This is the sentence they will remember. Say it once, precisely. Do not reach for poetry. The precision will be more moving than the metaphor.]

*Suggested length: 200–300 words.*

---

## The framework — and the chorus

*The research arrives here, after the body. It names what the practitioner and the natural body have already shown. It does not lead.*

[1–2 paragraphs. Draw on the relevant evidence base — neuroscience, creative health research, psychology, somatic theory — whatever the week calls for. Where a finding is contested, partial, or carries documented limitations, say so inside the prose, not as a bracketed aside. Honest uncertainty belongs in the same sentence as the claim it qualifies.]

**The chorus:**
[This is the move that makes the framework live. Bring in a second voice — from a different field, a different century, a different form of knowing — that is working on the same territory from another angle. Not to cite them, but to let them speak. What did they find? What would they say to the first thinker? What does their angle reveal that the first angle couldn't? The reader should feel that knowledge is alive and moving — not accumulated and stored.]

[Optional third voice, if it arises naturally. Never forced. The question is always: who else is in this room? Who was already working on this before the field had a name for it?]

*→ Integrity note: Every empirical claim must be traceable to a named source. If you cannot name the source, soften the claim or remove it. If a debate around a practitioner's work is unresolved, leave it unresolved.*

*→ On the chorus: the voices do not need to be from the same field, century, or discipline. A poet and a neurophysiologist may be working on the same problem. A 19th-century naturalist and a contemporary somatic therapist may have arrived at the same insight by different routes. Let them discover that in the reader's presence.*

*Suggested length: 300–400 words.*

## Into the studio

*Not an instruction. An invitation — from a peer, not a teacher.*

[3–4 sentences in first person. Describe your own starting point for this week's making. What does the natural body from this week make available to you? What are you not trying to achieve? Close with a line that releases the reader from obligation — they do not need to do what you are doing. They only need to find their own starting point.]

**What the hand might do:**

*These are entry points, not steps. Every body and every space is different. Adapt freely.*

**Before you touch anything:** Notice the weight of your body. Notice your breath. Name one thing your body is carrying right now.

**What you need:** Watercolour paper (or any absorbent surface that won't buckle), watercolours or whatever fluid pigments are available, water, a brush, somewhere to be undisturbed for twenty to forty minutes.

[3–5 short suggestions — each one names a gesture, not an image. What is the hand invited to do? Pool. Hold. Spread slowly. Let a colour stay longer than feels comfortable. Name the moment of resistance — when the watercolour does something the maker didn't plan — and what to do with it: stay, watch, let it be. End with: what has shifted? What does the body know now that it didn't at the start?]

*Time: 20–40 minutes, or as long as the body wants to stay.*

## Voice checklist

*Read the Make Space Voice Guide before you write. Return to it if you feel you've lost the thread. These questions are not a scoring rubric — they are a way of noticing where the writing is and isn't doing its work.*

Before you post:

- [ ] Did you read the Voice Guide before drafting?
- [ ] Is the key a single idea from a single thinker — not the week's topic restated, not a quote from the practitioner?
- [ ] Does the key make the reader feel something before they understand anything? Does it feel as though it was always true?
- [ ] Does the practitioner section begin in the middle of something specific — an action, a material, a session, a discovery?
- [ ] Does the natural body section describe what is actually true about that body, without reaching for metaphor?
- [ ] Does the chorus section let at least two voices — from different fields or centuries — speak to each other, rather than being cited in sequence?
- [ ] Does the framework arrive after the practitioner and the natural body — in service of what they've already shown, not ahead of it?
- [ ] Where research or theory is contested, is that said directly inside the prose — not as a bracketed aside?
- [ ] Is the studio invitation in first person, peer register — *I am starting here, you're welcome to come* — not instructional?
- [ ] Does the piece resist explaining what the practice means, and instead let it do its own work?
- [ ] Is there a single unifying question or tension that holds the whole piece together — felt rather than stated?
- [ ] Has every empirical claim been traced to a named source? Has every citation been verified against the actual text?
- [ ] Have any AI-generated biographical details, artist statements, or critical positions been verified against a primary source?
- [ ] Does the Rest section leave the week genuinely open — not a soft summary, but a real question the reader might carry?

## What this post is doing — held loosely, not as headers

1. **The key opens the door.** An idea from outside the field that makes this week feel necessary — felt before it is understood.
2. **The practitioner walks through it.** Something specific, real, and arresting that demonstrates the key without naming it.
3. **The natural body mirrors it.** Not as metaphor — as genuine companion, with its own biology and its own intelligence.
4. **The chorus makes it live.** Thinkers across time and field, in dialogue, discovering they were working on the same problem.
5. **The framework names it.** Lightly, in motion, with its limits already in it.
6. **The studio receives it.** An invitation, not an instruction. A starting point, not a destination.
7. **Rest leaves it open.** The field is still working this out. So are you.
