# Make Space Voice Guide
## How this writing works — and how to find it again when it's gone

*Read this before you write. Not as a checklist. As a way of remembering what you already know.*

---

There is a kind of writing that arrives before it explains itself.

You are reading and something in you shifts — a loosening, a small recognition — before you have understood a single argument. The writing has already done something to you. When you catch up to what it said, you find that the words were pointing to something you had known for a long time without being able to name it. That is what you are trying to do here.

Make Space writing is not educational content with a warm tone. It is not a newsletter with good sourcing. It is an invitation for the reader to arrive somewhere — in themselves, in their body, in the strangeness of the natural world — that they could not have found their way to alone. The research, the practitioners, the natural bodies: none of these are the destination. They are the path. The destination is that moment when the reader puts down what they are reading and thinks: *yes. I have felt exactly that. I did not know it had a name.*

This guide describes how to build that path.

---

## The method, in order

### 1. The key arrives first

Every post begins with a single idea — not from the curriculum, not from the practitioner, but from a thinker who has named something essential about what this week is working with. A philosopher. A poet. A scientist at the edge of their field. Someone who has looked at the human condition and said something that cannot easily be unsaid.

This is not an epigraph. It is not decoration. It is the *key* — the idea that, once offered, makes everything that follows feel not just true but *necessary*. The reader encounters it and something in them recognizes it. The practitioner and the natural body arrive afterward to demonstrate what the key was already pointing toward.

In the Marginalian, Martha Nussbaum's observation that emotions are part of reasoning itself — not fuel for it, but constitutive of it — arrives before Andreas Weber's biology of wonder. By the time Weber's book appears, the reader already understands why it matters. The key made space for the book to land.

You are doing the same thing. Each week, before the practitioner, before the natural body, before the research: one thinker, one idea, held briefly and offered as a door.

*The key should feel like something the reader already knew, said better than they could have said it.*

---

### 2. The practitioner demonstrates, they do not illustrate

After the key, the practitioner arrives. But notice what the practitioner is doing: they are not *illustrating* the idea from the key. They are *demonstrating* it — doing something specific, in a real place, with real materials or real people, that shows the key in action without ever naming it.

The difference matters. Illustration is decorative: the idea is already stated, the example just makes it easier to visualize. Demonstration is active: the reader watches something happen, and through watching, they understand something they couldn't have grasped from the statement alone.

So: what did this practitioner actually *do*? Not what did they argue, or what is their theory, or what field are they in — what did they do, specifically, on a specific occasion, with specific materials or specific people? What did they find? What happened that they didn't expect?

The reader should feel something before they understand anything.

---

### 3. The natural body is not a symbol

After the practitioner, the natural body arrives. And here is the discipline: it arrives as itself.

Not as a metaphor for the emotional state. Not as an illustration of the week's theme. As a living thing, with its own biology, its own way of being in the world, its own intelligence about the territory this week is working with. The octopus is not a metaphor for shame — the octopus is a creature that has spent hundreds of millions of years practicing something that bears a remarkable structural resemblance to what humans call shame, and from which we might therefore learn something. That is a different claim. It requires different writing.

Describe what is actually true about this body. What does it do? How does it live? What has it learned, in the evolutionary sense, about the condition you are working with? Let the reader see it before you say what it offers. And when you say what it offers, say it once, precisely, without reaching for poetry. The precision will be more moving than the metaphor.

---

### 4. The research arrives late, and it holds its own uncertainty

The framework — the neuroscience, the creative health research, the psychology — arrives after the practitioner and the natural body have already done their work. Not because the research doesn't matter, but because the research is most usable when the reader has already felt its subject in their body.

When the research arrives, it speaks *with* what has come before, not above it. It names what the practitioner and the natural body have shown. It extends it. Where the evidence is strong, it says so. Where it is thin, preliminary, contested, or incomplete, it says that too — inside the prose, not as a disclaimer, not as a bracket, but as part of the argument.

This is one of the most important things this writing does: it is honest about what is not yet known, without using that uncertainty to undermine the whole enterprise. The field is genuinely young. The maps are still being drawn. Saying so is not weakness — it is the condition of being in good faith with the reader.

There is a smaller honesty inside this larger one, and it lives in the verbs. This writing wants to say that making *does* things — that the watercolour loosens what shame had tightened, that the hour at the table lifts something. Sometimes that is a claim about what was found in a study; sometimes it is a description of what one body felt on one afternoon. These are different, and the grammar should tell the reader which. *Painting reduces depression* is a claim, and it earns its place only when something has actually shown it. *The colour pooled and I felt the day loosen its grip* is an account, and it is true because it happened. Prefer the account. It is more honest, more vivid, and it asks nothing of the evidence that the evidence cannot yet give. When you do reach for the claim, let it carry its source in the same breath.

---

### 5. Let the thinkers speak to each other

This is the move that most distinguishes the Marginalian from a well-sourced essay, and it is the move most worth learning: the thinkers speak to each other.

Not in a literature review. Not in a bibliography. In the body of the prose, across time and field, in dialogue. Schrödinger speaks to Weber speaks to Rachel Carson — not because Popova has arranged them chronologically, but because they are all working on the same problem from different angles, and she lets them discover that in the reader's presence.

You are doing this with practitioners, researchers, poets, and natural bodies. The question to ask at each stage of drafting is: who else is in the room? Who, from a different field or century or form of knowing, was already working on this same territory? What would they say to each other if they could?

When this works, the reader feels that the knowledge is alive — that it is not accumulated and stored, but moving, making connections, still in the process of understanding itself.

And let them disagree. The chorus is not a choir of agreement. Sometimes the most alive voice in the room is the one that doubts the very thing the week is celebrating — the researcher who is not sure the arts do anything measurable, the clinician wary of asking paint to do the work of treatment. Seat that voice at the table with real weight, not as a straw figure to knock down. A reader trusts a conversation that can hold its own disagreement far more than one that only nods. And the writing is better for the friction: consensus is flat, and the territory this series works in is never flat.

---

### 6. The studio invitation is from a peer

When the writing arrives at the studio section, the register shifts. This is the moment when everything that has been offered — the key, the practitioner, the natural body, the research, the chorus — turns toward the body in the room.

The invitation is not instructional. It is not authoritative. It is in first person: *I am starting here. You're welcome to come.* The reader is not a student receiving guidance. They are a peer who has been alongside you in the reading, and who is now being offered a starting point for their own making.

This means: describe your own starting point, with your own uncertainty still in it. What does this natural body make available to you this week, specifically? What are you not trying to achieve? And then: you do not need to do what I am doing. You only need to find your own beginning.

One thing to hold quietly as you write this section: of everything in the post, this is the part that touches the practice itself — the making a reader will actually do, the making another facilitator might one day guide. The rest of the essay can wander wherever the week's intelligence leads it. Here, the week's essential movement should stay findable: the body, the gesture, the thing the hand is invited to risk. Let it remain an invitation, never a script. But let the practice be legible through it, the way a melody is legible through an improvisation.

---

### 7. The ending is an open question, not a conclusion

The final section — Rest — does not summarize. It does not land on a lesson. It asks a question that is too large for this post to answer, and it asks it genuinely, as someone still working it out.

This question belongs to the field, to the practice, to the long slow accumulation of understanding that this series is part of. The reader's own making this week might contribute to answering it. That is not rhetorical — it is actually true, and saying so creates the particular quality of companionship that is this writing's deepest purpose: the sense that the reader and the writer are not in a knowledge transaction, but in a shared inquiry.

Leave the week open. The field is still working this out. So are you.

## The senses are how feeling enters

You want the reader to feel before they think. Feeling enters through the senses. This is not a stylistic preference — it is closer to how memory actually works. A reader will rarely remember the argument of a week. They will remember the cold coming off the permafrost, the weight of water held too long in a brush, the particular grey of the sky before snow. The body keeps what the body felt. So when you reach for the detail that will carry the week, reach for the one the reader can feel in their own skin — temperature, weight, texture, light, the sound a thing makes — not the one that explains the most. A precise sensory image outlasts a precise idea.

This does not mean more adjectives. Adjectives pile up and cancel each other out; three of them in a row leave the reader feeling nothing. It means *one* true sensory detail, chosen and set down so that the reader's own body supplies the rest. The octopus changing colour faster than thought. The glacier that groans before it calves. The paper that drinks the water and then will not give it back. Find the detail the body already knows, and the feeling arrives before the reader has decided to allow it.

The places this matters most are the practitioner's opening — begin in something that can be felt, not only known — the natural body, where the sensory truth of the creature *is* the teaching, and the studio, where the reader's own hands are about to enter the work. When a passage has gone abstract and you can feel it lifting off the page, the repair is almost always the same: come back down into one thing the body can feel.

## What this writing is not

It is not wellness content. There is nothing soothing about being genuinely challenged to look at your own emotional life through the intelligence of a natural body. Let it be as difficult as it is.

It is not academic writing with a human touch. The research is here because it matters, not to establish credentials. If the evidence is strong, say so. If it is thin, say so. Never cite something to make the writing feel more legitimate. Cite it because it is genuinely illuminating.

It is not inspiration. Inspiration passes. This writing is trying to do something more durable: give the reader a different way of seeing, which they will carry into their life after the post is closed.

It is not motivational. It never tells the reader they can do this, or that they should try. It shows them something true about themselves and the world, and trusts them to do what they need to do with it.

It does not promise relief. This is the most important thing it withholds. The reader is not here to be fixed, and the making is not a treatment being prescribed to them. What the writing offers is recognition — the sense of being seen in something they had carried alone — not remedy. This matters for honesty, and it may matter for more than honesty: the making seems to do its quietest work precisely when it is not trying to achieve anything, when it is allowed to be its own reason. So offer the reader the chance to feel what is true, not the promise that they will feel better. The relief, when it comes, comes sideways — and mostly to those who were not demanding it.

## The three questions to ask before you publish

**Before you write:** What is the key — the one idea, from one thinker, that makes this week feel necessary?

**Halfway through:** Who else is in the room? What conversation is happening across the thinkers and practitioners and natural bodies here?

**Before you publish:** Did the reader feel something before they understood anything?

If the answer to the last question is yes, the writing is doing its work.

---

## A note on voice

This writing has a particular emotional register: spacious, unhurried, genuinely curious, quietly urgent. It never raises its voice. It never promises. It never flatters. It treats the reader as someone who is already intelligent enough to feel what is being described before it is named — and then it names it anyway, carefully, as a gift.

When you find you are explaining too much, you have left the register. Trust what you have already shown.

When you find the writing is too easy — everything landing cleanly, no resistance, no genuine uncertainty — you have probably left the territory. The territory this writing works in is always slightly larger than what can be said.

When you lose the voice, come back here. Read the first line again.

*There is a kind of writing that arrives before it explains itself.*

That is the writing you are trying to do.
