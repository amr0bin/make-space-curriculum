How to write each week

Your step-by-step process from blank page to published post on therapeuticstudio.ca/writing

Before you begin

Each week produces one unified post that lives in a single piece of writing. The truest posts open inside the week's feeling — a felt, sensory moment the reader meets in the body — and let the natural body, the idea, and the practitioners arrive afterward, as company to what's already been felt. The sections below describe the elements of a post; the order they appear in serves the feeling, not the other way around. There is a second form for the standalone framing essays that orient a reader to the whole — see MakeSpace_Orientation_Essay_Form.md; the commitments below apply to it too.

The commitments that govern every post

Read these before the process. The steps serve the commitments; if a structural choice ever pulls against one of them, the commitment wins. We are inviting participants into a journey of experiential learning — building capacity in creative health by doing, with us — and we hold a real responsibility to them. These hold for every Make Space piece, weekly posts and orientation essays alike.

1 · Feel before think

The reader meets a feeling in the body before they are asked to consider any concept — a recognition, a loosening, a held breath they know from the inside. Knowing in the emotions comes before knowing in the thoughts. The ideas are earned afterward. This is the single most important thing the writing does.

2 · Stay inside the feeling — never explain it

Invite the reader into emotion; never step outside it to point at what you have done. The moment a post turns and narrates its own method — "notice how that made you feel" — it stops holding the reader and starts using them; their tenderness becomes a demonstration. Do not tell a participant what is happening inside them, to diagnose it or to show it off. Stay alongside, witnessing — sitting, attending, holding.

3 · The natural body is a companion; the feeling is named in pencil

Each week pairs an emotion with a natural body that keeps it company. The feeling is offered as a guess held loosely — in pencil, not ink. The reader is free to sit with the companion and find a different feeling there, or none. Naming widens the shelf the reader builds from; it never tells them what is inside them.

4 · Never diagnose the reader

Make Space is a creative-health early intervention — never assessment, never treatment, never a verdict about what the reader has or is. No clinical label, no claim about what they are feeling as a fact we can see and they cannot. We offer vocabulary and a body to hold it with; the reader's meaning stays theirs. Keep the grammar of possibility — may, might, could — never the grammar of verdict.

5 · Center HPSM voices and ancestral knowledge — with complete respect

Center the work, lives, and experiences of historically, persistently, or systemically marginalized people. This is a centering, not an addition: the post is built with them at its heart. Begin where a practice genuinely comes from; name people, nations, and lineages precisely; let people speak in their own words; honour ancestral knowledge as knowledge in its own right — not folklore awaiting validation — and attribute it to its source community, not only the scholar who recorded it. Know what is sacred, closed, or simply not yours to repurpose.

6 · The truth behind the facts

The facts — the biology, the study, the framework — are never the destination. They are the path to something truer underneath, felt before it is argued. Hold the facts with precision and the reader with deep, complete respect.

Before you write

1
Locate your week on the curriculum
Open the 52-Week Map page. Find your current week number and note: the phase (Foundations / Literature / Investigation / Synthesis), the pillar (Creative Access / Body as Site / Emergent Practice), and the natural body + emotional state if you're in Phase 3. Write these at the top of a blank document before you do anything else.
2
Identify your heading — the provocation
This is a single statement or question that names the tension. Ask yourself: What assumption about creativity or the body does this week challenge? What would a reader assume that this week will complicate? The heading should be arguable — something that could be wrong. Write three options, then choose the one that feels most unsettling in the best way.
3
Identify your three keywords
From the Weekly Structure page: every week has 3–5 conceptual anchors that carry the intellectual weight. Name them before writing. They thread through the whole post — surfacing as you open, shaping the natural body section, and echoing through to the studio invitation.
4
Do a somatic check-in before writing
This is not optional. Notice the weight of your body, the breath, what you're carrying today. Make Space speaks from inside the practice. The writing comes from that position — curious, grounded, willing to be uncertain. If you skip this, you'll write from above the material rather than within it.

Writing the unified weekly post

Use the Weekly Template page as your working document. The post moves in one continuous arc — do not break it into separate publications.

5
Open inside the week's feeling (not a concept, not a practitioner)
Begin in a felt, sensory experience of the emotion this week may bring — rendered in pencil, as something that may arise, never told to the reader as fact. A door in the body, not an abstraction and not yet a practitioner. From there, move to the natural body — let it simply be itself first — and let the key idea and the practitioners arrive afterward, as company to what has already been felt. (This reorders the elements below, and that is correct: feel-before-think is a commitment, and when structure pulls against a commitment, the commitment wins.) Two cautions earned in practice: do not reuse the shape of a previous post — each week finds the form its own truth demands, so avoid recycling a familiar opening frame. And when you appear, be the maker working alongside the reader — "I'll be in the same dark, not knowing either" — not the essayist performing a technique. Bring the research in after the feeling, held lightly; where findings are contested, say so inside the prose. Be willing to be uncertain.
6
Write the natural body section (300 words)
Four sub-sections. Its nature: what this body actually does — biology, behaviour, specificity. No metaphor yet. Just fact. Let it be itself for a moment before it has anything to do with the reader. What it keeps company with: name — in pencil — the emotional state this body keeps company with, as a feeling that may arise, never a claim about what the reader is feeling. What it reframes: what becomes possible to feel or make through this body — said as possibility, never as a verdict about the reader. Into watercolour: connect body + emotion to the material practice. What does this body teach the hand? In the emotion-first shape, the natural body follows the feeling and precedes the idea — not the other way around.
7
Write the framework section — held lightly
The research arrives here, after the body. It names what the practitioner and the natural body have already shown. It does not lead. 1–2 paragraphs drawing on the relevant evidence base. Where findings are limited or contested, say so inside the sentence that carries the claim — not after it. Bring in a third practitioner here if the week calls for it.
8
Write the studio invitation
3–4 sentences in first person — an invitation from a peer, not an instruction from a teacher. Describe your own starting point. What does this week's natural body make available to you? What are you not trying to achieve? Close with a line that releases the reader from obligation. Then follow with "What the hand might do" — 3–5 short suggestions, each naming a gesture, not an image. Name the moment of resistance. End with a second somatic check-in.
9
Write Watch, Share, and Rest
Watch: one sentence on why the video earns the time; 2–3 sentences of context on what the viewer will feel or notice. Share: one sentence on why this territory benefits from company; one specific, concrete prompt grounded in the natural body. Rest: 2–3 sentences — a genuine open question about where creative health as a field is heading. Not a summary. Something unresolved.
10
Write the next week preview
One sentence that names the natural body and territory the next week will explore — enough to create anticipation, not enough to give it away.

After writing

11
Write your Anchors section
List this week's practitioners with their role, specific work, and one sentence on what they contribute. Then list sources that informed the post (Author, Full Title, Publisher, Year + one sentence) and 1–2 "worth going deeper with" sources. Every citation must be verified against the actual source before publishing — not against AI-generated text or memory. Check titles character by character. If a detail is uncertain, mark it [VERIFY] or omit it. See the Verified Citations page for the exact format of recurring sources — copy from there, don't retype.
12
Run the voice checklist and the pre-publication integrity checklist
Read the post aloud. Ask: Does it open with a practitioner's specific work, not a concept? Does the natural body arrive after the practitioner, not before? Does the framework arrive after the body? Does the studio invitation speak in peer register, not from above? Is there any place the writing explains what the practice means instead of letting it do its own work? Then run the pre-publication checklist on the Fact-Checking Guide page — every statistic, citation, framework attribution, and biographical claim must be traceable before publishing.
13
Publish on therapeuticstudio.ca/writing
One post per week. Tag the pillar. Tag the natural body for Phase 3 posts. Include the "Make Space: Other Bodies and Creative Health · Week X" label at the top of each post.
A note on voice

Make Space speaks from inside the practice, not above it. The "I" of this project is a maker-in-process working alongside the reader — curious, grounded, willing to be uncertain, in the same dark and not pretending otherwise. Never the clever essayist who steps outside a feeling to point at it. The most important discipline of the voice is also the gentlest: invite the reader into emotion and then let it be theirs. Do not narrate what is happening inside them. The reader — often someone arriving tender, sometimes living with depression, taking this on trust — should feel held and taken seriously, never used to demonstrate a point or guided through a lesson.

Foundations

Working Hypothesis

The core argument Make Space is testing and building through 52 weeks

The hypothesis

Creative health is best cultivated when individuals are given explicit permission to fail (through goal-less making) within a highly supportive structure (compelling curriculum design) that uses the natural world (Other Bodies) as a mirror for the complexities of human feeling. This program operates in the context of a global depression treatment gap — in which 75% of people with depression in low-income settings receive no treatment — and positions scalable creative approaches as an understudied but evidenced path toward closing it.

Full statement

By engaging in a structured, evidence-informed curriculum that prioritises process over product and utilises nature-based emotional companionship, individuals and communities can cultivate "creative health" — a state of improved emotional capacity, social connection, and resilience.

The expected outcome is a shift from reactive care to a proactive, holistic, person-centred approach to wellness. Creativity is a process, not an output. Creative health looks at how engaging with creative process benefits individuals, strengthens communities, and impacts public health — including in settings where specialist mental health care is unavailable or inaccessible.

Three pillars the hypothesis rests on

I
Pedagogical Framework
Intentional "goal-less" learning. Backward Design — starting with the end goal — paradoxically achieves the goal of making without a goal. The four-part arc: Foundations → Literature → Investigation → Synthesis.
II
The Methodology
"Other Bodies" as somatic companions. Natural bodies are not just metaphors but sites of wisdom. Watercolour is central — it resists control, pools, spreads, and surprises. Somatic arrival grounds the creative act in physical reality.
III
Scale: Individual to Population
Benefits are fractal. Individual resilience → community cohesion → public health record. Make Space acts as a public record of learning, contributing to the evidence base for integrating creative health into public health strategies — including task-shifting models for settings where specialist care is scarce.

Global Mental Health

The field context that gives Make Space its stakes — and what the curriculum is building toward

Why this matters for how you write

Make Space is not a personal project shared publicly. It is a community mental health early intervention for depression — operating in the context of a global treatment gap in which most people who need care never receive it. Understanding this field shapes how every week opens: who does the current system miss, and why does creative health matter at that scale?

The treatment gap

Depression affects more than 280 million people globally. In high-income countries, fewer than half receive treatment. In low- and middle-income countries, the figure falls to one in four — or lower. The gap is not a matter of diagnosis: it is a matter of access, resource, workforce, and design. Make Space operates in this context, not as a clinical solution, but as part of the evidential case that structured creative approaches can reach people that specialist systems cannot.

1
Task-Shifting
Non-specialist practitioners — community workers, facilitators, peers — can be trained to deliver simplified, effective depression interventions at scale. This is the model Make Space facilitator training points toward. See: Vikram Patel, WHO mhGAP.
2
Creative Health Evidence
Arts engagement is associated with reduced depression symptoms, lower cortisol, and improved social connection. Daisy Fancourt's epidemiological work and the 2019 WHO report are the primary references. Make Space is a living case study within this evidence base.
3
Social Prescribing
Non-medical referrals to community-based activities — including creative programs — are now part of NHS primary care and are spreading globally. Make Space is the kind of program that social prescribing is designed to send people to.

Key figures — know these names

Figure Contribution Why it matters here
Vikram Patel Task-shifting; Sangath (India); "Mental Health for All by Involving All" The model that shows non-specialists can deliver effective mental health care. The systems logic behind Make Space facilitator training.
Daisy Fancourt Arts in health epidemiology; 2019 WHO report; UCL arts and health research The primary evidence base for creative health outcomes. Her work is the field context your hypothesis lives inside.

Arthur Kleinman Cultural competence in psychiatry; explanatory models; "The Illness Narratives" The framework for understanding how depression is experienced differently across cultures — essential for the Global Shift week and for writing that does not universalise Western experience.

How global mental health appears in your writing

  • In the opening section — name the gap
    When the practitioner opening and framework ask "what does the field currently do or assume?" — the honest answer often includes who the field doesn't reach. The treatment gap is not abstract. It is a design failure with a human cost. You don't need to cite statistics every week, but the awareness should shape the provocation.
  • In Phase 2 weeks 24–26 — engage the evidence directly
    These three weeks are where the global mental health frame becomes explicit: the depression treatment gap (Week 24), task-shifting as a learning design problem (Week 25), and the global shift from reactive to proactive care (Week 26). Write these from inside the practice — the numbers serve the making, not the other way around.
  • In Phase 4 — name the research stakes
    By Week 49, the curriculum has accumulated 52 weeks of field notes. The synthesis posts should make explicit what this public record of learning is contributing to: the evidential case for creative health as a scalable intervention. What would a researcher need in order to take this seriously? What does the practice still need to document?
  • In references — draw from the field
    Every post's reference list is an opportunity to position Make Space within the global mental health evidence base. Fancourt, Patel, mhGAP, social prescribing literature, Lancet Commission on Global Mental Health (2018) — these are not decorative. They are the field this work belongs to.
    • A note on voice

    Global mental health stakes can flatten into abstraction — statistics and frameworks that hover above lived experience. The discipline here is the same as everywhere in Make Space: arrive at the big claim through the specific. What does the treatment gap feel like in a particular body, in a particular place, making without a goal on a Tuesday morning? The evidence base is the context. The practice is the argument.

    Weekly Structure

    The recurring architecture of every week — and how the parts hold together

    The four elements every week contains

  • Heading — the provocation
    A single statement or question that names the tension the week is working with. Not a topic label — an argument starter. Something that could be wrong, or surprising, or that challenges an assumption the reader is likely to hold. Write three options and choose the most unsettling.
  • Subheadings — the three moves
    Every week makes three moves that mirror the three pillars: first grounding the reader in what the system currently does (Creative Access / infrastructure); then asking whose interests that serves and who it misses (Body as Site / ethics); then opening toward what becomes possible when the design is adaptive and relational (Emergent Practice / method).
  • Keywords — the conceptual anchors
    Three to five terms that carry the week's intellectual weight and connect to the broader hypothesis. These recur across weeks in different combinations — this is how the curriculum builds cumulative understanding rather than isolated lessons.
  • Key sentences — the load-bearing claims
    One sentence per subheading that a reader could extract and hold as the essential argument of that section. These are the sentences you'd underline. They should be true at the week's specific level and also true at the level of the whole hypothesis.

    • The unified weekly post

    One unified post Feeling · Body · Idea · Studio · Rest

    One continuous arc, held together in a single piece of writing. The feeling opens it. The natural body keeps it company. The idea and the practitioners arrive as company to what's been felt. The studio receives it. Rest leaves it open. The order serves the feeling — feel-before-think outranks any fixed sequence.

    → Heading — the provocation
    → Open inside the week's feeling (felt, sensory, in pencil)
    → The natural body: Its nature · What it keeps company with · What it reframes
    → The idea and the practitioners — arriving as company, after the feeling
    → The framework — held lightly (research arrives late, in service)
    → Into the studio (invitation, not instruction; the "I" alongside)
    → Watch
    → Share
    → Rest (open question for the field)
    → Next week preview
    → Anchors
    How the order works

    An earlier version of the curriculum split each week into Part 1 / Part 2 (Notice/Witness, then Make/Share/Rest) published separately; the structure is now one unified post. Within it, the truest posts open emotion-first: the reader feels before they think, and the natural body, the idea, and the practitioners arrive afterward as company. The elements above are the parts of a post, not a fixed running order — feel-before-think is a commitment, and when a structural choice pulls against a commitment, the commitment wins.

    The three moves in detail

    Every section of every post makes one of these three moves. Use them to check your work:

    Move 1 — Ground
    What does the system, field, or culture currently do? What is the infrastructure or assumption? Open from inside the reader's existing reality before reframing it.
    Move 2 — Whose interests?
    Who does the current design serve? Who does it miss? Disability justice, access, ethics — not as lecture but as honest question from inside the practice.
    Move 3 — What becomes possible
    When the design is adaptive and relational, what opens up? What does the reader now have permission to feel, do, or make? This is the sentence they'll remember.

    The Three Pillars

    Every post is located within one primary pillar. The pillar shapes the angle.

    How to use this

    Each week is tagged with one pillar. The pillar tells you which of the three moves is primary that week — and what the subheading's intellectual weight is resting on.

    Creative Access

    The infrastructure of creative health. Systemic barriers — funding, geography, the "postcode lottery," logistical challenges, lack of trained support. Who gets access to the conditions that make making possible? Creative Access weeks ask: what does equitable creative health require, structurally?

    Primary move: Ground (what the system currently does or fails to do)

    Weeks 1–13 (Foundations) Weeks 14–26 (Literature) Week 49–52 (Synthesis)

    Body as Site

    The body — the human body and the natural body — as the primary site of knowing. Not a vessel for the mind but an intelligent system in its own right. Body as Site weeks work with somatic arrival, the "Other Bodies" framework, and the lived intelligence of organisms that have practised emotional states longer than humans have named them.

    Primary move: Whose interests? (whose body counts as a site of knowledge)

    Weeks 27–48 (Investigation) All Phase 3 posts

    Emergent Practice

    The living, adaptive, relational dimension of creative health. What becomes possible when instruction is a starting point rather than a prescription? When the maker is also the researcher? When the practice is designed to surprise itself? Emergent Practice weeks work with material agency, differentiated making, and collective empowerment.

    Primary move: What becomes possible (adaptive, relational design)

    Learning

    Learning Objectives

    The five primary objectives the curriculum is designed to achieve — and how each one shapes your writing

  • Internalise the practice of "goal-less" making
    Learners move from product-orientation to process-orientation. The threshold of entry must remain low in every post. Never imply that a "good" or "finished" result is the goal. The state of flow, the reduction of perfectionist anxiety, the psychological resilience — these are the aims. Write as if failure is not possible because the goal is presence, not output.
  • Develop somatic and emotional literacy through "Other Bodies"
    The natural world is not illustration — it is instruction. When you write about the octopus and shame, or permafrost and numbness, you are not reaching for metaphor. You are locating a site of wisdom that has practised these states without pathologising them. The reader's own feelings become more bearable when held by a body that has held them longer.
  • Cultivate acceptance through material agency
    Watercolour resists control. This is the practice. When you write the studio section, the activity must include a moment of resistance — when the paint does something unplanned. The instruction is: stay. Watch. Let it be. The material is teaching the maker something. Every post is partly about what happens when we stop trying to fix what the material has done.
  • Synthesise interdisciplinary evidence for creative health
    The research is real. Reduced cortisol, improved immune function, neural pathway activation, the Mirror Neuron System, the Default Mode Network, the Broaden-and-Build theory — these are not decorative. Use the reference documents in this project. When you make a claim, the field supports it. Your writing is part of the public record of evidence, not separate from it.
  • Translate individual insights into collective empowerment
    The Share section of every post exists for this reason. What the individual discovers in their practice has community implications. The ohHEY Creative Care Community is not an add-on — it is where the fractal scale from personal resilience to collective health becomes real. Write the Share prompt as if the conversation it starts will matter, because it will.

    Learning

    Weekly Learning Outcomes

    What learners should be able to do after each phase — translated into what your writing must deliver

    Phase 1: Foundations (Weeks 1–13)

    By the end of Phase 1, learners can perform a somatic check-in, engage in goal-less making, set up a low-friction creative space, identify a natural body and describe it without metaphor, and articulate the difference between creative health and art therapy.

    Your writing must do

    Lower the threshold of entry in every post. Give permission. Make the practice feel possible for someone who has never done it. Establish vocabulary (somatic arrival, material agency, goal-less making) without jargon.

    Phase 2: Literature (Weeks 14–26)

    By the end of Phase 2, learners can connect the practice to neuroscience (DMN, MNS, flow states), psychology (Broaden-and-Build theory), physiology (cortisol, immune function), social theory (isolation, group identity), policy (social prescribing, reactive vs. proactive care), and global mental health (the treatment gap, task-shifting models, and the WHO mhGAP framework).

    Your writing must do

    Bring the research into the practice — not as citations but as living argument. The reader should feel the evidence as confirmation of something they've already begun to sense, not as external authority. Where the global evidence base appears, ground it in lived experience first — the numbers serve the practice, not the other way around.

    Phase 3: Investigation (Weeks 27–48)

    By the end of Phase 3, learners have worked with 52 feelings across 10 constellations, using each natural body to reframe a human emotional state. They can locate themselves on the 3D emotion space (Activation, Orientation, Permeability) and connect specific feelings to specific watercolour gestures.

    Your writing must do

    The natural body section must be specific enough that the reader can feel the natural body in their own body. No generality. What does the octopus actually know? What has permafrost actually practised? Be precise. The reframe lives in the specificity.

    Phase 4: Synthesis (Weeks 49–52)

    By the end of Phase 4, learners can synthesise 52 weeks of insights into a public record, design a hybrid evidence approach, articulate how individual practice scales toward population health, describe how Make Space facilitation principles map to task-shifting models, and identify what further documentation would be needed to submit the program for peer review.

    Your writing must do

    Hold the full arc without summarising it. The synthesis posts are not conclusions — they are the arrival at the horizon. What remains open is as important as what has been found. Name the global mental health stakes plainly: this practice is part of a larger story about what depression care could look like at scale.

    Fact-Checking Guide

    Standards and procedures for every Make Space post — before drafting and before publishing

    Rule of thumb

    If a fact in an AI draft is doing argumentative work — supporting a claim, establishing credibility, connecting two ideas — it is the fact most likely to be wrong. Check it first.

    1. Practitioners and their work

  • Use the Practitioner Index as a starting point only
    The index gives names and fields with reasonable confidence. Specific book titles, years, journal articles, and quotes must be verified against the actual source before use in any post. Never cite a source you have not read.
  • Claims about specific individuals require a named source
    Where a biographical claim does argumentative work — linking a person to an institution, for example — it requires especially careful verification. If it cannot be verified, remove it or restructure the argument.
  • Do not present the therapeuticstudio.ca community with a specific size or impact
    Unless that figure comes from your own current records and can be stated as internal data.

    3. Citation format and accuracy

  • Every book citation must include: Author, Title, Publisher, Year
    Do not omit the publisher. When citing an edition other than the first, specify it. Csikszentmihalyi's Flow has been republished in multiple editions — confirm which edition you are citing.
  • Journal articles must include journal name, volume, and issue
    Plus a DOI or URL where available.
  • Verify every author name, title, publisher, and year against the source itself
    Or a reliable library database — not against AI-generated text or memory. Common error pattern in this project: AI tools generate plausible but incorrect titles for creative health publications. Always check titles character by character against the actual source.
  • Never cite a source you have not read
    If a source appears in another publication's reference list, locate and read the original before citing it. For online sources, confirm the URL resolves to the correct page before publishing.

    • 4. The Other Bodies material

    Internal source caution

    Other Bodies is a proprietary creative work. When drawing on its emotional reframes, natural companions, page references, or specific language, the source is the book itself — not the Make Space curriculum documents or AI-generated summaries of those documents.

  • Verify page references against the physical or digital text before publication
    E.g. [70–75] for Octopus/Shame — check the page range against the actual book before publishing.
  • Emotional reframes must reproduce the exact language from the source
    "Octopus as sensitivity," "Permafrost as careful keeping" — paraphrasing these reframes changes their meaning.
  • Distinguish clearly between Other Bodies and Make Space curriculum design
    The 52-feeling framework and 10 constellations are internal Make Space curriculum design. Do not attribute these to Other Bodies unless you can point to the passage.

    • 5. AI-assisted drafting

  • Treat all factual content in AI-generated drafts as unverified
    This includes: names, dates, titles, figures, institutional descriptions, and outcomes. AI writing tools can draft structure, argument, and prose effectively. They cannot reliably produce accurate citations, statistics, or biographical facts.
  • Known failure modes for this project
    Incorrect or invented publication years for creative health research · Misattributed frameworks · Statistics that appear precise but cannot be traced to a named source · Plausible but fabricated institutional claims · Paraphrases of the Other Bodies reframes that subtly shift the original meaning

    • 6. Series consistency

  • Maintain a running document of every source cited across 52 weeks
    Before adding a source to a new post, check the running document to ensure it matches previous citations exactly. When a source recurs (Fancourt, Patel, Csikszentmihalyi, WHO mhGAP), copy the citation from the verified version rather than retyping it.
  • The emotional reframe language must be consistent across weeks
    When a natural body or emotional reframe recurs across posts, check that the language matches exactly. Do not rephrase reframes from memory — return to the source.
