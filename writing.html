How to write each week

Your step-by-step process from blank page to published post on therapeuticstudio.ca/writing

Before you begin

Each week produces two posts: Part 1 (Notice, Witness) and Part 2 (Make, Share, Rest). Both live within the same natural body and emotional territory. Write Part 1 first — it generates the material Part 2 needs.

Before you write

1
Locate your week on the curriculum
Open the 52-Week Map page. Find your current week number and note: the phase (Foundations / Literature / Investigation / Synthesis), the pillar (Creative Access / Body as Site / Emergent Practice), and the natural body + emotional state if you're in Phase 3. Write these at the top of a blank document before you do anything else.
2
Identify your heading — the provocation
This is a single statement or question that names the tension. Ask yourself: What assumption about creativity or the body does this week challenge? What would a reader assume that this week will complicate? The heading should be arguable — something that could be wrong. Write three options, then choose the one that feels most unsettling in the best way.
3
Identify your three keywords
From the Weekly Structure page: every week has 3–5 conceptual anchors that carry the intellectual weight. Name them before writing. They will appear in your Notice section, shape the Witness section, and echo across both parts.
4
Do a somatic check-in before writing
This is not optional. Notice the weight of your body, the breath, what you're carrying today. Make Space speaks from inside the practice. The writing comes from that position — curious, grounded, willing to be uncertain. If you skip this, you'll write from above the material rather than within it.

Writing Part 1 — Notice, Witness

Use the Part 1 Template page as your working document.

5
Write the Notice section (250–350 words)
Three moves: (a) What does the field / culture / system currently do or assume? (b) What concept reframes that? — draw on the Make Space hypothesis. (c) Close with the load-bearing claim for this section: one sentence a reader could underline. Write from inside the practice. Use "I". Be willing to be uncertain.
6
Write the Witness section (300–400 words)
Four sub-sections. Its nature: what this body actually does — biology, behaviour, specificity. No metaphor yet. Just fact. What it holds: name the emotional state, explain how the natural body carries it as lived condition. What it reframes: what changes for a human when they look at their own feeling through this body — what becomes possible. Into watercolour: connect body + emotion to the material practice. What does this body teach the hand?
7
Write the closing question
One genuinely open question — not answerable by the post you just wrote. It should point toward the edge of what is known. What is the practice still working out? What might the reader be positioned to discover?
8
Check: load-bearing sentences
Re-read Part 1. Find the one sentence per sub-heading that a reader could extract and hold as the essential argument. Underline it. If you can't find it, the section needs a revision pass. These are the sentences you build the curriculum's cumulative understanding from.

Writing Part 2 — Make, Share, Rest

Use the Part 2 Template page. Part 1 must be drafted first.

9
Write the threshold sentence
One sentence that reconnects to last week's natural body and signals the shift from understanding to making. Not a recap — a threshold. Something like: "Last week we sat with what [natural body] knows about [emotional state]. This week we let it move the hand."
10
Write the Make section and activity
One paragraph naming the creative act and connecting it to the natural body + emotional state. Then: numbered step-by-step instructions. Begin with somatic check-in. End with somatic check-in. Give the gesture, not the image. Name the moment of resistance. Write for someone who has never made without a goal before — no art background, no particular emotional readiness. Only a body and a willingness to begin.
11
Add the Watch, Share, and Rest sections
Watch: one sentence introducing the video; 2–3 sentences of context. Share: one sentence on why this emotional territory benefits from community; one specific, concrete prompt — not "share your experience" but something grounded in the natural body. Rest: 2–3 sentences — a genuine open question about where creative health as a field is heading. Not a summary. Something unresolved.

After writing

12
Write your references
List sources that informed the post (Author, Title, Year + one sentence on why it matters here). Add 1–2 "worth going deeper with" sources. The reference documents in this project — the comprehensive overview, individual/community impact papers — are your primary evidence base. Draw from them, don't just cite them.
13
Final read: voice check
Read both posts aloud. Ask: Does this sound like a maker-in-process or like a teacher delivering a lesson? Is there any sentence that explains rather than holds? Any place the writing steps above the practice rather than moving inside it? Make those cuts.
14
Publish on therapeuticstudio.ca/writing
Post Part 1 first. Part 2 can follow in the same week or the week after. Tag the pillar. Tag the natural body for Phase 3 posts. Include the "Make Space: Other Bodies and Creative Health · Week X, Part Y" label at the top of each post.
A note on voice

Make Space speaks from inside the practice, not above it. The "I" of this project is a maker-in-process — curious, grounded, willing to be uncertain. The reader should feel taken seriously, not guided through a lesson.

Foundations

Working Hypothesis

The core argument Make Space is testing and building through 52 weeks

The hypothesis

Creative health is best cultivated when individuals are given explicit permission to fail (through goal-less making) within a highly supportive structure (compelling curriculum design) that uses the natural world (Other Bodies) as a mirror for the complexities of human feeling.

Full statement

By engaging in a structured, evidence-informed curriculum that prioritises process over product and utilises nature-based emotional companionship, individuals and communities can cultivate "creative health" — a state of improved emotional capacity, social connection, and resilience.

The expected outcome is a shift from reactive care to a proactive, holistic, person-centred approach to wellness. Creativity is a process, not an output. Creative health looks at how engaging with creative process benefits individuals, strengthens communities, and impacts public health.

Three pillars the hypothesis rests on

I
Pedagogical Framework
Intentional "goal-less" learning. Backward Design — starting with the end goal — paradoxically achieves the goal of making without a goal. The four-part arc: Foundations → Literature → Investigation → Synthesis.
II
The Methodology
"Other Bodies" as somatic companions. Natural bodies are not just metaphors but sites of wisdom. Watercolour is central — it resists control, pools, spreads, and surprises. Somatic arrival grounds the creative act in physical reality.
III
Scale: Individual to Population
Benefits are fractal. Individual resilience → community cohesion → public health record. Make Space acts as a public record of learning, contributing to the evidence base for integrating creative health into public health strategies.

Key claims — hold these when you write

Claim What it means in practice
Natural bodies are sites of wisdom, not illustrationThe octopus doesn't symbolise shame — it has actually practised something humans call shame for longer than we have. Write from that position.
Watercolour resists control — and that is the pointMaterial agency is pedagogical. When the paint does something unplanned, staying with it is the practice.
Somatic arrival is not optionalThe creative act must be grounded in physical reality before emotional exploration begins. Every session starts and ends with a somatic check-in.
Process over product — every timeNo post should ever imply that a "good" outcome is the goal. The threshold of entry must remain low for every reader.
Making without a goal is evidence-basedFlow states, cortisol reduction, emotional regulation — the field supports this. You are working within a research context, not against it.

Weekly Structure

The recurring architecture of every week — and how the parts hold together

The four elements every week contains

  • Heading — the provocation
    A single statement or question that names the tension the week is working with. Not a topic label — an argument starter. Something that could be wrong, or surprising, or that challenges an assumption the reader is likely to hold. Write three options and choose the most unsettling.
  • Subheadings — the three moves
    Every week makes three moves that mirror the three pillars: first grounding the reader in what the system currently does (Creative Access / infrastructure); then asking whose interests that serves and who it misses (Body as Site / ethics); then opening toward what becomes possible when the design is adaptive and relational (Emergent Practice / method).
  • Keywords — the conceptual anchors
    Three to five terms that carry the week's intellectual weight and connect to the broader hypothesis. These recur across weeks in different combinations — this is how the curriculum builds cumulative understanding rather than isolated lessons.
  • Key sentences — the load-bearing claims
    One sentence per subheading that a reader could extract and hold as the essential argument of that section. These are the sentences you'd underline. They should be true at the week's specific level and also true at the level of the whole hypothesis.

    The three moves in detail

    Every section of every post makes one of these three moves. Use them to check your work:

    Move 1 — Ground
    What does the system, field, or culture currently do? What is the infrastructure or assumption? Open from inside the reader's existing reality before reframing it.
    Move 2 — Whose interests?
    Who does the current design serve? Who does it miss? Disability justice, access, ethics — not as lecture but as honest question from inside the practice.
    Move 3 — What becomes possible
    When the design is adaptive and relational, what opens up? What does the reader now have permission to feel, do, or make? This is the sentence they'll remember.

    Each week is tagged with one pillar. The pillar tells you which of the three moves is primary that week — and what the subheading's intellectual weight is resting on.

    The infrastructure of creative health. Systemic barriers — funding, geography, the "postcode lottery," logistical challenges, lack of trained support. Who gets access to the conditions that make making possible? Creative Access weeks ask: what does equitable creative health require, structurally?

    The body — the human body and the natural body — as the primary site of knowing. Not a vessel for the mind but an intelligent system in its own right. Body as Site weeks work with somatic arrival, the "Other Bodies" framework, and the lived intelligence of organisms that have practised emotional states longer than humans have named them.

When the practice is designed to surprise itself? Emergent Practice weeks work with material agency, differentiated making, and collective empowerment.

    Learning

    Learning Objectives

    The five primary objectives the curriculum is designed to achieve — and how each one shapes your writing

  • Internalise the practice of "goal-less" making
    Learners move from product-orientation to process-orientation. The threshold of entry must remain low in every post. Never imply that a "good" or "finished" result is the goal. The state of flow, the reduction of perfectionist anxiety, the psychological resilience — these are the aims. Write as if failure is not possible because the goal is presence, not output.
  • Develop somatic and emotional literacy through "Other Bodies"
    The natural world is not illustration — it is instruction. When you write about the octopus and shame, or permafrost and numbness, you are not reaching for metaphor. You are locating a site of wisdom that has practised these states without pathologising them. The reader's own feelings become more bearable when held by a body that has held them longer.
  • Cultivate acceptance through material agency
    Watercolour resists control. This is the practice. When you write the Make section, the activity must include a moment of resistance — when the paint does something unplanned. The instruction is: stay. Watch. Let it be. The material is teaching the maker something. Every Part 2 post is partly about what happens when we stop trying to fix what the material has done.
  • Synthesise interdisciplinary evidence for creative health
    The research is real. Reduced cortisol, improved immune function, neural pathway activation, the Mirror Neuron System, the Default Mode Network, the Broaden-and-Build theory — these are not decorative. Use the reference documents in this project. When you make a claim, the field supports it. Your writing is part of the public record of evidence, not separate from it.
  • Translate individual insights into collective empowerment
    The Share section of every Part 2 post exists for this reason. What the individual discovers in their practice has community implications. The ohHEY Creative Care Community is not an add-on — it is where the fractal scale from personal resilience to collective health becomes real. Write the Share prompt as if the conversation it starts will matter, because it will.

    Learning

    Weekly Learning Outcomes

    What learners should be able to do after each phase — translated into what your writing must deliver

    Phase 1: Foundations (Weeks 1–13)

    By the end of Phase 1, learners can perform a somatic check-in, engage in goal-less making, set up a low-friction creative space, identify a natural body and describe it without metaphor, and articulate the difference between creative health and art therapy.

    Your writing must do

    Lower the threshold of entry in every post. Give permission. Make the practice feel possible for someone who has never done it. Establish vocabulary (somatic arrival, material agency, goal-less making) without jargon.

    Phase 2: Literature (Weeks 14–26)

    By the end of Phase 2, learners can connect the practice to neuroscience (DMN, MNS, flow states), psychology (Broaden-and-Build theory), physiology (cortisol, immune function), social theory (isolation, group identity), and policy (social prescribing, reactive vs. proactive care).

    Your writing must do

    Bring the research into the practice — not as citations but as living argument. The reader should feel the evidence as confirmation of something they've already begun to sense, not as external authority.

    Phase 3: Investigation (Weeks 27–48)

    By the end of Phase 3, learners have worked with 52 feelings across 10 constellations, using each natural body to reframe a human emotional state. They can locate themselves on the 3D emotion space (Activation, Orientation, Permeability) and connect specific feelings to specific watercolour gestures.

    Your writing must do

    The Witness section must be specific enough that the reader can feel the natural body in their own body. No generality. What does the octopus actually know? What has permafrost actually practised? Be precise. The reframe lives in the specificity.

    Phase 4: Synthesis (Weeks 49–52)

    By the end of Phase 4, learners can synthesise 52 weeks of insights into a public record, design a hybrid evidence approach, and articulate how individual practice scales toward population health.

    Your writing must do

    Hold the full arc without summarising it. The synthesis posts are not conclusions — they are the arrival at the horizon. What remains open is as important as what has been found.
